\textbf{Question}:\\
\begin{align}
    \lim\limits_{x\to\infty} \frac{x-\sin x}{x+\cos x} 
\end{align}
equals
\begin{enumerate}
    \item $1$
    \item $-1$
    \item $\infty$
    \item $0$
\end{enumerate}
\textbf{Solution: }
The minimum and maximum of the numerator $x-\sin x$ is $x-1$ and $x+1$. The minimum and maximum of the denominator $x+\cos x$ similarly, is $x-1$ and $x+1$. 
\begin{align}
    \frac{x-1}{x+1} &< \frac{x-\sin x}{x+\cos x} < \frac{x+1}{x-1} \\
    \lim\limits_{x\to\infty} \frac{x-1}{x+1} &= \lim\limits_{x\to\infty} \frac{x+1}{x-1} = 1 \text{ (By L'Hospital)}
\end{align}

So by sandwich rule,
\begin{align}
    \lim\limits_{x\to\infty} \frac{x-\sin x}{x+\cos x}  = 1
\end{align}

\begin{center}
    \boxed{\textbf{Option A is the answer: Limit is 1}}
\end{center}
